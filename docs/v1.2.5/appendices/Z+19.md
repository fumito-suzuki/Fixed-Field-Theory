## 3. Main Derivation / Model

### 3.1 C-Waves Carry No χ

For all C-waves, including neutrinos,

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{C\text{-}wave}} \equiv 0.
$$

C-waves are pure interference structures and become observable only if they
successfully interact with a T-wave receptor.

Neutrinos obey this rule exactly.

### 3.2 Neutrinos as the Extreme Low-δ Limit

Neutrinos are characterized by:

- extremely long effective wavelength,
- extremely low interference density,
- near-rectilinear propagation.

Formally,

$$
\delta_{\nu} \ll \delta_{\gamma},
$$

where $\delta_{\nu}$ denotes neutrino interference density and
$\delta_{\gamma}$ that of photons.

As a consequence:

- diffraction is negligible,
- scattering is negligible,
- phase distortion is minimal.

Neutrinos therefore propagate almost perfectly straight.

### 3.3 Failure of ψ-Generation in Matter

An observable event in FFT requires stable ψ-generation:

$$
\psi = \chi_{\mathrm{T}} \, \delta_{\mathrm{C}}.
$$

For neutrinos,

$$
\psi_{\nu} =
\chi_{\mathrm{matter}} \, \delta_{\nu}
\approx 0.
$$

Even in dense matter, the interference density is too small to generate stable
ψ.

Thus:

- no ψ is formed,
- no Z-update occurs,
- no observation is registered.

This is the precise FFT meaning of “neutrinos pass through matter.”

Neutrino detection occurs only when

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{environment}} \, \delta_{\nu}

This requires:

- extremely dense media,
- very large interaction volumes,

In such cases:

This manifests experimentally as rare flashes or scattering signatures.

### 3.5 Near-Light-Speed Propagation

In FFT, C-wave propagation speed depends on interference density:

$$

c \left( 1 - \epsilon(\delta) \right),
\qquad
\epsilon(\delta) \to 0
\;\text{as}\;
\delta \to 0.
$$

Since neutrinos satisfy $\delta_{\nu} \approx 0$,

$$
v_{\nu} \approx c.
$$

Their speed is therefore not a consequence of small mass, but of **nearly
interference-free propagation**.

Because neutrino C-waves:
